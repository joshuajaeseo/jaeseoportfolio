DSGN 308

Make grocery bags stronger and sustainable.


From personal experience our team had identified weak paper grocery bags as a problem space, but the insights from grocers using the bags on a daily basis piqued our interest.

Our user research among Evanston-area grocery shoppers confirmed that this was a problem space worth exploring.

(Along the way we utilized some unconventional methods to collect user data.)

The product requirements of durability, sustainability, comfort, and economics were drawn from our surveys, user interviews, and patent research.

Through ideation our team created four prototypes.

The prototypes were tested against single bags and double bags: common use cases in the real world.

To test the bags we found a grocery load that was on the breaking point of a single bag.

With this predetermined load we subjected each type of bag to this circuit of activities increasing in intensity.

The objective of the tests were to find the point of structural failure for each bag; no bag would be able to survive every single test.

The breaking points were then plotted. This showed us that the double handle bags and masking-taped handle bags performed the best out of the four prototypes.

From these tests two frontrunners emerged, but although the double-handle bags performed the best, the increased complexity in manufacturing compared to the relative simplicity of manufacturing tape-style stickers to paste over handles led to sticker-handle bags as the final product.

If our team decides to pursue this project further, we have outlined the steps needed to be taken to take our product to market.
